## Overview

The Náhuatl Dictionary (`DictionaryScreen`) helps you learn indigenous language terms used in the stories. It's both a cultural resource and an educational tool for preserving the Náhuatl language.

<Note>
The dictionary is accessible from the Discover tab. Tap the "Diccionario Náhuatl" card to open it.
</Note>

<Steps>

### Example Entries

**Kuauhtli** (Águila / Eagle)
- Important symbol in Mesoamerican culture
- Represents strength and connection to the sky
- Appears in many legends and place names

**Tonal** (Sol / Sun)
- Central to indigenous cosmology
- Associated with life, energy, and time
- Used in many story titles and phrases

**Chikawakatl** (Mujer fuerte / Strong woman)
- Compound word: chikawa (strong) + katl (woman)
- Used in legends about powerful female figures

<Note>
The dictionary reflects the specific Náhuatl dialect spoken in the Sierra de Zongolica, which may differ from other Náhuatl variants.
</Note>

## Learning Features

### Pronunciation

Náhuatl pronunciation can be challenging:

- **Vowels**: a, e, i, o are pronounced as in Spanish
- **Consonants**: Some differ from Spanish (e.g., "tl" is a single sound)
- **Stress**: Usually on the penultimate syllable
- **Length**: Long vs. short vowels can change meaning

### Common Patterns

**Compound words**: Náhuatl often combines words:
- **Kuauhtli** (eagle) + **tonal** (sun) = **Kuauhtonalli** (eagle of the sun)

**Suffixes**: Common endings:
- **-tl**: Singular noun ending
- **-meh**: Plural ending
- **-ka**: Locative (at/in a place)

<Note>
The dictionary helps you recognize these patterns, making it easier to understand unfamiliar Náhuatl words in stories.
</Note>

## Cultural Context

The Náhuatl language:

- **Family**: Uto-Aztecan language family
- **Speakers**: Approximately 1.7 million speakers in Mexico
- **Variants**: Many regional dialects
- **History**: Language of the Aztec empire, still widely spoken
- **Status**: Recognized as a national language in Mexico

### Sierra de Zongolica Dialect

The Zongolica region has its own Náhuatl variant:

- **Central Nahuatl**: Related to other Central Mexican variants
- **Unique vocabulary**: Some words specific to the Sierra
- **Living language**: Actively spoken in daily life
- **Oral tradition**: Strong tradition of storytelling in Náhuatl

<Note>
The dictionary reflects real language use in the Sierra de Zongolica communities, not just academic or standardized Náhuatl.
</Note>

## Troubleshooting

<Accordion title="Can't find a word">
  - Try alternate spellings (Náhuatl orthography varies)
  - Search for root words (compound words may not be listed separately)
  - Check if the word is in a different section or category
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Pronunciation doesn't match audio stories">
  Different narrators may have regional accents or pronunciation variations. Both are valid.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="How do I learn Náhuatl beyond individual words?">
  The dictionary is a starting point. Consider:
  - Reading bilingual stories in the app

  - Taking formal Náhuatl language classes
  - Engaging with Náhuatl-speaking communities
</Accordion>
